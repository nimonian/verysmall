Indefinite integration

Recall from C1 that we may integrate a function $f(x)$ to give a new function $F(x)$.
In symbols, we write: \[ \int f(x) \, dx = F(x) + c \]
Importantly, the derivative of this function $F(x)$ is $f(x)$, so \[F^{\prime}(x) = f(x) \]

Integrating terms in index form

The rule for integrating $x^{n}$ is $$ \int x^{n} \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + c$$ This still works even when $n$ is not a positive integer! \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{x\sqrt{x}} \, dx & = \int x^{-\frac{3}{2}} \, dx \qquad \left(n=-\frac{3}{2}\right) \\ & = x^{-\frac{1}{2}} \div \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) + c \\ & = -2x^{-\frac{1}{2}} + c \\ \end{align*}

Definite Integration

Once the integral has been given limits of integration , we can substitute them to give a numeric answer. This is called definite integration . In general notation, we write: $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) $$ The number $a$ is the lower limit of integration, and the number $b$ the upper limit.

You may remember that, if \(f(x) \geq 0\) for $a < x < b$, then $\int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx$ represents the area under the curve $f(x)$ between $a$ and $b$.

Improper Integrals

Sometimes, we want to integrate a function all the way along the $x$-axis to $\infty$ or $-\infty$ (for example, in probability). This is the first type of improper integration. Improper integrals with infinity look like $$ \int_{a}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx \qquad \text{or} \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{b} f(x) \, dx $$ They are worked out by first finding $\int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx$ and then imagine either $a$ or $b$ going to $\pm \infty$

Although this sounds crazy, it's not always difficult.

The most important things to remember

For $k>0$, as $ b \rightarrow \infty $, $$ \frac{1}{b^{k}} \longrightarrow 0 \qquad \text{but} \qquad b^{k} \longrightarrow \infty $$
However, as $ b \rightarrow 0 $, $$ \frac{1}{b^{k}} \longrightarrow \infty \qquad \text{but} \qquad b^{k} \longrightarrow 0 $$


If you're not sure, substitute $b=1000$ or $b=0.001$ into your integreated expression to see if your answer makes sense.

Improper intgerals with $0$

The second type of improper integral is when $a=0$ or $b=0$, which looks like this: $$ \int_{a}^{0} f(x) \, dx \qquad \text{or} \qquad \int_{0}^{b} f(x) \, dx $$ The problem occurs when $f(x)$ is not defined (i.e. has an asymptote) at $x=0,$ for example $f(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}$

However, they can be worked out using the same trick as we use for $\pm\infty$

Summary

  • Indefinite integration is when no limits are given: $\int f(x) dx $
  • Improper integration is when one (or both) of the limits is $\infty$ or $0$
  • Swap the improper limit for $a$ or $b$ and then analyse what happens as they go to $\infty$ or $0$
  • You must write your limiting statment before your final answer, e.g. as $b\rightarrow 0 \, \ldots $
