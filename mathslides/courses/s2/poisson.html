S2 Poisson Distribution

Objectives:

What does it do?

The Poisson distribution is a discrete probability distribution, similar to the binomial distribution.

It is used to find the probability of the number of events happening within a given timeframe, such as the number of cars which pass a hotel each hour, or the number of phonecalls received by a hotel every week.

We need to be familiar with the conditions for a distribution to be valid.

Conditions

A Poisson distribution can only be used when the events:

  • are random
  • are independent
  • occur at a uniform average rate

The phrase uniform average rate is a key phrase in this topic and it is often used to justify why the Poisson distiribution should or should not be used.

Examples

Here are some situations which may be modelled by the Poisson distribution:

  • The number of bombs landing on a particular area of a battlefield
  • The number of insurance claims made by homeowners per week
  • The number of white blood cells in a blood sample
  • The number of printing errors in a newspaper

Non-Examples

If we change the situation slightly, then the Poisson distribution may not apply:

  • The number of bombs landing on an area of a whole country
  • The number of insurance claims during a flood
  • The number of white blood cells from a sample taken near a wound
  • The number of spelling mistakes made by a human

The formula

The probabilities for the Poisson distribution are given by the formula: \[ P(X=r) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^{r}}{r!} \] where \( \lambda \) is the mean or expected value. It is not important to fully understand this formula yet, but you should be able to substitute into it on your calculator.

The number \( r \) is the number of times we want something to happen (e.g. \(r\) could be the number of bombs, insurance claimes, white blood cells or mistakes, etcetera).

The values for \(r\) can be chosen infinitely large.
